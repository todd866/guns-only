# Guns Only — Design Spec

*2026-07-17 · working title "Guns Only" (rename at will) · v4 — Taiwan Strait canon. Strips the Korean-War/human-pilot residue that survived v3's jet-drone reframe (the aircraft, sights, damage model and milestones were still written as a Korean-War human-pilot game underneath a drone-fiction skin), downgrades several "derived" claims that were actually design choices, fixes one factual error (AWACS escorts), and folds in what flying the M0 slice found. Supersedes v3 (2026-07-16), which reframed to jet drones after live feel-testing but never finished carrying that reframe through the rest of the document · v2 was adversarially reviewed (4 Claude lenses + Codex) and those hardenings are preserved · status: in build (M0); the feel gate itself has not yet been flown.*

## 1. Vision

A flat-screen, laptop-native, **guns-only 1v1 jet-drone dogfight**, set in a **near-future Taiwan Strait conflict**, flown on a **full aerodynamic model** through a novel **doctrine-detent keyboard control scheme**, presented first-person through the drone's **sensor feed** with a glass combat HUD, and debriefed like a real sortie.

**One sentence:** the vibes of dogfighting a jet, on the machine you already own, with no joystick, no switchology, and no missiles of your own — in a war neither side has fought yet.

**The thesis (why this game exists, stated plainly):** this project exists to show what the *next* war looks like, not to build a high-fidelity museum piece of the last one — Falcon 4.0 modeled its F-16 to the rivet and never modeled the war it would actually fly in. The setting is Taiwan's defense against the PLA, and the cost-exchange / attritable-mass / kill-the-enablers argument below *is* the asymmetric-defense argument, not a coat of fictional paint over a generic dogfight game. That argument lives at two altitudes with different jobs:

- **The strategy layer's job is to be TRUE.** Cost-exchange, magazine depth, delivery-as-escalation-axis, fleet-in-being (§3a) — these have to survive contact with how a real blockade, real PLA doctrine, and real economics actually work. Where the fiction guesses or simplifies, this spec says so, out loud, rather than asserting it as settled.
- **The dogfight's job is to be FUN.** BFM, guns, sight-fighting, the doctrine-detent grammar — none of that needs to be *true*, it needs to be *good*, and it stays honest on its own terms (pillar 1: the physics never lies) without needing to also carry the strategic argument on its back.
- **The dogfight is the gateway drug to the argument.** Nobody sits down to absorb a thesis about asymmetric-defense economics. They sit down to dogfight. The thesis is what's still there after the fiftieth sortie, once you've noticed you're always the cheap thing, and it always costs the other guy more to be there.

**Why drones (a design choice with real payoffs, not a free lunch):** the gameplay is *derived from the physics*, so the fiction is mostly a skin — energy, pursuit geometry and gun solutions fall out of aerodynamics that don't care whether there's a pilot aboard, so two different fictional dressings over the same FDM produce close to the same BFM. That's a real property of the physics. But v3 oversold it by calling the drone choice *free* ("touches no code"): removing the pilot changed real code, and for the better — the G-limit went from a physiological cap to a structural one (§5), the view became a gimbaled sensor with honest lost-tally arcs instead of an eyeball (§8), and the external chase camera was deleted from the codebase outright, not merely left unused (§8). Those changes match the grammar's own logic ("bare arrows can never depart" always implied a limiter with no human physiology behind it) — they just weren't free, and claiming they were is exactly the kind of overclaim this revision exists to fix. What the drone choice does still pay for cleanly: no cockpit-art dependency (the view is a sensor feed, which is what the HUD already was), a purely structural G-limit that matches the grammar's premise, and fictional airframes (no licensing, no rivet-counters, no switchology).

**Why guns (the design's load-bearing argument — one strong leg, two supporting ones, not a proof):**
1. **Cost-exchange.** A $2M interceptor against a $50k drone loses the war economically even while winning engagements. At scale you need a ~$10-per-shot weapon. That's a gun. This is the strongest leg — it *is* the asymmetric-defense thesis above, not a gameplay excuse dressed up as one.
2. **Magazine depth.** 2–4 missiles versus hundreds of rounds implies dozens of passes, and in an attrition war depth beats per-shot quality — as an argument, not a proof. Missile inventories aren't physically fixed; a determined-enough procurement effort could buy more missiles instead of guns. This leg *supports* the choice; it doesn't compel it, and v3 wrote it as if it did.
3. **Un-jammable.** Mature counter-seeker EW collapses missile Pk; you cannot jam a bullet. Passive EO/IR sensor + gun is a kill chain that plausibly survives heavy EW — which is also the in-fiction reason the view is a passive sensor feed, and why *seeing him* is a real mechanic.

Read together, these make guns-only a well-motivated design choice. They do not make it inevitable — a different, equally defensible fiction could still carry missiles. Say so plainly rather than dressing a choice up as a derivation.

**Who the player is: an AI — a deliberate design choice, not something the premise forces.** The EW argument above (seeker denial) would, pushed hard enough, also degrade a datalink to a remote human operator — a genuinely nice piece of internal consistency, and the actual reason we made the pilot an onboard AI rather than a human on the loop. But it's an argument *for* the choice, not an entailment *of* it: a sufficiently resilient datalink isn't physically impossible, it's just a less interesting game, and v3's "the premise forces it, it isn't a flourish" overstated that. Made honestly as a choice, it still pays off three things already built:
- **The HUD is not an instrument — it's attention.** An AI wouldn't read a pitch ladder; but what the sensor feed and glass symbology *depict* is what the mind is attending to. The TD box is attention on the bandit; the ladder is proprioception. The UI is the character's phenomenology.
- **The detent grammar is a model of mind.** The valleys are not an assist and not an autopilot being supervised — they are **reflexes** (System 1). The player is the deliberative layer (System 2) modulating off its own instincts, and the override key (§7) is the mind overruling its own envelope protection.
- **The debrief is you reviewing your own death** (§11), and `R` is not a restart but the **next instance**. The retention loop (daily seeded fight, "beat your own grade") becomes successive minds flying the same problem and learning.

This also gives the Tier-2 manned fighter (§3a) its real payload: not "cheap beats expensive" but a $50k expendable *mind* against a $100M protected *body*, and the question of which the war actually values. Written as premise; it required real code (§5, §8) to make honest, and the *delivery* (stated flatly vs. revealed) stays a narrative problem for later.

**An interesting convergence, not the setting:** removing the pilot removes the physiological G cap, but induced drag scales with G², so *sustained* turn performance stays thrust-limited, not structure-limited — you can snap a harder instantaneous break; you cannot hold it (§5 has the actual numbers, measured, not asserted). Add economics — cheap ⇒ light structure ⇒ transonic, not supersonic — and a derived airframe family lands in roughly the same envelope as 1950s transonic fighters, from cost-and-aerodynamics arithmetic alone, with zero historical input (§4). That's a genuinely interesting result, worth keeping on record. It is not the setting: the aircraft in this game are fictional near-future jet drones, the theater is the Taiwan Strait, and the war is not Korea. Treat the convergence as a footnote about how constrained the design space is, not a costume.

**Product hypothesis (evidenced, not proven — see `docs/research/2026-07-16-prior-art-survey.md`):** a guns-only pure-BFM game with an honest energy model exists and is loved in VR (Virtual Fighter Maneuvers, 95% positive on a small review base). The survey found no flat-screen equivalent: no shipped game combines a real energy model + guns-first BFM + a first-class flat-screen view answer. This project combines them, and adds two things the survey found nowhere: a non-cheating doctrine-driven AI and a control scheme designed for a laptop rather than ported to one. Note the survey's own finding that the healthiest games in this niche (Nuclear Option, VFM) use **fictional airframes** — the drone reframe lands us there deliberately.

### Design pillars (tie-breakers for every future decision)

1. **The physics never lies.** Player and AI fly the same model; energy is never faked; the sight is honest.
2. **Delegate the stick-waggling, never the decisions.** Automation handles execution precision; every tactical choice — pursuit geometry, the forks, the shot — belongs to the player. Operational test: **if the debrief would annotate it as a choice, the detent may never make it.**
3. **Losing sight must be scary — for both sides.** The sight fight is the emotional core; no magic sensors for either aircraft.
4. **The whole fight, nothing but the fight.** Merge → BFM → guns → debrief. Anything that isn't that is out.
5. **Anti-study-sim.** Zero mandatory switchology. If a control exists, it matters in the fight.

## 2. Non-goals (v1)

- **You never carry missiles — ever (identity, not scope).** Note the clarification: *guns-only describes YOUR loadout, not the threat environment.* Facing incoming missiles is not a contradiction — it is the entire reason the cost-exchange argument exists — but modelling them (seekers, RWR, notching, countermeasures) is a whole milestone and is **roadmap, not v1** (§3a).
- No campaign, no multiplayer, no VR, no HOTAS requirement (HOTAS may *work* later; it is never assumed). Scripted **drills/missions** are permitted only where they reuse existing machinery and close a known gap — see the Tier-0 drill in §3a.
- No clickable systems depth: the only mid-fight interactions are ones that matter in the fight (sensor ranging, gimbal/padlock, flaps/boards).
- No cockpit interior, no takeoff/landing in v1 — the view is the drone's nose sensor feed and fights start airborne.
- **No gimbaled gun.** A drone *could* gimbal its gun, and doing so would delete BFM entirely (fly past, shoot sideways, no geometry). We take the fixed-forward-gun branch deliberately, defensibly (gimbal mass/cost/ammo-feed on an attritable; boresight rigidity; recoil through the CG) — but it is a **choice**, not something the derivation forced. Written down so nobody re-derives it away later.
- **The detents are the control scheme, non-optional** (see §7). In-fiction they are the drone's own flight AI; the player is the tactical brain on the loop.
- **The detents are the control scheme, non-optional.** The assist slider (§7) fades the *suggestion UI* only. There is no "raw mode" to balance against (the rejected autonomy dial stays rejected).

## 3. Player experience — the loop

**Setup → Fight → Debrief → again.**

- **Setup screen:** choose jet (Type 1 or Type 2, §4), starting geometry (offensive perch / defensive perch / neutral merge / butterfly), altitude/airspeed presets, **fuel preset (the round timer)**, AI tier. One screen, launch in seconds.
- **Fight:** the 1v1, from setup until one of the **defined endings** below. Typical duration 1–5 minutes.
- **Debrief:** replay with flight-path ribbons, energy traces, doctrine annotations, gun camera. `R` restarts instantly.

**Fight endings — every one has an owner and a score:**
- **Kill** (or mutual, or a lost airframe on unrecoverable departure — there's no one aboard to eject; an unrecoverable departure just ends the fight as a loss).
- **Hard deck** (briefed floor): bust it and the fight ends as a loss annotation — exactly like the training rules the sortie is modeled on.
- **Bingo:** the fuel preset reaching bingo forces the AI to disengage honestly and ends the fight; scored from the continuous advantage metric (§11).
- **Auto knock-it-off:** N seconds of neutral separation beyond X km ends the fight as a draw-with-grade. Player can also call KIO manually (`K`).
- The AI's willingness to accept risk **rises as fuel falls** (a doctrine-engine term), so two conservative aircraft converge instead of orbiting a lufbery forever.

**Flight school (onboarding, in-architecture):** 6–10 canned drills, each one P-825 atom — hold lag, convert the overshoot, defend guns, work the ranging brackets — flown against the scripted drone with the doctrine annotations *live* instead of post-hoc. Plus a one-screen concept card per doctrine term (one-circle, lag, corner speed…), linked from the debrief the first time each term appears. The suggestion UI teaches *what to press*; flight school teaches *why*; the debrief teaches *what happened*.

**Retention hook (v1, nearly free given determinism):** a **daily seeded fight** — same setup + AI seed for everyone that day, shareable debrief — and a graded sortie score computed from the doctrine engine's continuous advantage metric ("beat your own BFM grade"). Longer term, a thin strategic layer — blockade posture shaping which tactical setups are on offer (§3a) — is the intended answer to keeping this fresh past the daily-fight novelty; that's M2+ and not designed yet.

The whole game honors a 10-minute laptop session: lid open → fighting inside 30 seconds.

## 3a. The threat ladder and delivery (roadmap spine — ORDER MATTERS, see §15.7)

The fiction: near-future Taiwan defending against the PLA across the strait. Every tier below is a real mission category in that fight, not a difficulty reskin, and it supplies a difficulty ramp without a campaign. **None of this precedes the M0 feel gate**; it is banked here so it isn't lost, and ordered so it can't sprawl (the niche's named killers are CAP2's decade of execution starvation and TCA's content drought — the antidote is ordering ideas, not refusing them).

**The ladder teaches one pillar per tier — and it fell out of the fiction rather than being designed top-down, which is why it's better than a bolted-on tutorial:**

- **Tier 0 — junk-drone hunting (Shahed-types) → teaches GUNNERY.** Slow, non-maneuvering, defenceless. Closure, ranging, burst discipline — the gun's whole language learned somewhere that cannot kill you. **This is the onboarding fix** for the review's finding that nothing teaches before the first twenty deaths, and it is **nearly free**: `RailBandit` already flies scripted non-maneuvering targets on a timeline. Cheapest high-value item on the roadmap; build it when flight school (§3) is built.
- **Tier 0.5 — the glider strike (balloon → altitude → a PLA enabler) → teaches ENERGY, and teaches it better than anything else in the game.** Strip the engine and you strip the thing that papers over energy mistakes: with no throttle, **every turn is a withdrawal from an account you can never pay back into.** Physically honest: at ~70k ft you're above ~96% of sea-level density (a huge energy account, almost no q to spend it with — you go straight and build speed), a decent L/D buys hundreds of km of range, a dive goes transonic; no engine means no IR plume and no intake return, which is *why* it can reach the target undetected. Doctrinally the right kind of target: enablers are enormous and cannot dodge — but **"undefended" overclaimed it, and this revision fixes that.** Real PLA KJ-500 patrols fly with J-10/J-11/J-16 fighter escorts and surface warships; an isolated intercept is not the standing picture. Beat 4 (below) currently models an isolated target as a simplification the fiction has to *earn* — an escort gap, a diversion, degraded coverage — not assert as a standing fact. Modeling an escort/SEAD layer is real future work and is undesigned.
  - **What flying it found (bin/mission, headless):** the original Beat 4 geometry had a 140 kg engineless glider trying to *tail-chase* a 253 kt KJ-500 — kinematically impossible; minimum range occurred at t=0 and only grew. The real mission is a **diving slashing pass**: trade 10,000+ ft of altitude for 400–500 kt, take one fleeting firing window, and don't try to re-attack — you have no engine to set up a second pass. The original 70,000 ft balloon drop also handed the glider ~340 km of glide energy against an ~8 km intercept problem, so an early build arrived at 426 kt with a 2 km turn radius and blew past the target at 3 km, 64° off. **The mission's real skill is energy *disposal*, not marksmanship** — this is M2 content, not solved, and the beat needs redesigning around the slashing-pass geometry rather than patched around the tail-chase one.
- **Tier 1 — gun-drone BFM → teaches GEOMETRY.** The core game. Everything else in this spec. As of 2026-07-17, 2 of the 3 canned M0 beats never converge to a firing solution — the hand-authored execution laws fly toward the bandit but don't close to a tracking window. That's not a bug; it's exactly the gap the M3 doctrine engine exists to close (§14).
- **Tier 2 — the manned 4th/5th-gen fighter.** Thematically the payoff: you are the cheap thing, he is the $100M thing, and you win by economics rather than superiority. **The framing that saves this from becoming a swarm-AI project:** he already spent his missiles on the four drones ahead of you — you're the fifth. Now it's guns, 1v1, and *he* is the one who can't afford to trade. Say plainly what this is: a **contrived setup**, not a systemic guarantee — a real pilot who's expended his entire missile load before merging with a fifth cheap drone is an authored coincidence engineered to buy a clean 1v1 payoff without multi-ship AI. The honesty cost is worth paying; it's still a contrivance. Modelling *incoming* missiles (seekers, RWR, notching, countermeasures) is a real milestone in its own right and stays behind the core fight.

### Delivery — an escalation axis, not just a cost axis

v3 treated delivery as a single mechanism (balloon drop solves the runway/range problem). It's better understood as a **ladder of escalation**, because each method trades deniability against usability, and that tradeoff is itself part of the strategic argument this game exists to make (§1):

- **Balloon — deniable, sub-threshold, works pre-war, unkillable, slow and wind-dependent.** Details below.
- **Sea-drone / USV launcher — mobile, dispersed, survives day one.** A fixed launch site is the first thing a precision strike removes; a launcher that's also a boat solves the survivability problem a runway or a silo cannot.
- **Rocket lob EAST, at the blockade — legitimate.** Trajectory resolves away from the mainland; nothing about an eastward launch reads as an attack on China. This is the usable half of the capability.
- **Rocket lob WEST, at the mainland — cheapest on paper, unusable in practice.** A westward launch is indistinguishable from an ICBM on early-warning radar until the terminal seconds. This isn't a targeting problem, it's an escalation-management problem: the delivery method with the best physics (shortest range to target) is the one you can never actually use, because the launch itself risks triggering a strategic response calibrated to a completely different kind of weapon.
- **Fleet-in-being.** The rocket capability's value is mostly in its existence: it shapes PLA dispersal and forces defensive posture without a shot ever being fired. This is the strategy-layer's "TRUE" half of the thesis (§1) — the tactical dogfight never needs to render it, but a setup screen or mission briefing can gesture at it.
- **Japan (Tanegashima) as a plausible launch state.** Routine eastward launches from Japanese territory don't trip alarms the way a launch from Taiwan or off China's coast would — space-launch traffic there is unremarkable background noise. This is speculative fiction-building for the strategy layer, not a claim about actual Japanese policy.

**Balloons, in detail.**
- **Hydrogen, not helium.** A blockaded Taiwan cannot import helium; hydrogen is water plus electricity, so it's the one delivery method that keeps working *during* a blockade, which is the entire point of choosing it.
- **Mass is brutally levered.** On one 20 m hydrogen balloon, a 140 kg glider reaches ~81,000 ft where an 1,100 kg airframe only reached ~41,000 ft. Going lighter buys altitude — and therefore glide range — far faster than it costs capability, once the mission is mission-kill sized rather than destruction sized (see Glider, below).
- **Wind — verified, with real corrections needed.** A winter westerly at Taiwan's latitude broadly supports west-to-east balloon flight, and this isn't invented: the PRC flew 83 balloons from Fujian eastward across the strait between Dec 2023 and Feb 2024, so this fiction inverts a real, recent campaign — Taiwan launching the same trick back. Three things need flagging honestly rather than smoothed over:
  - 23°N sits in a **QBO/extratropical transition zone**, so the seasonal window is **QBO-phase-dependent** (the Quasi-Biennial Oscillation cycles roughly every 28 months) — not a clockwork season you can rely on every winter.
  - The "launch from Central Asia" claim that circulates in balloon-campaign write-ups is **unverified at the 17–24 km altitudes** these operations actually use — it was inferred from a *tropospheric* jet-splitting pattern, not a stratospheric one, and shouldn't be treated as established.
  - Google's Loon project proved balloons can be **steered by altitude-layer selection** — different stratospheric layers carry different, sometimes opposing wind directions, and a balloon that can change altitude can ride a favorable layer without an engine.
  - **Canonical framing for the game:** the controlling variable is **altitude-layer selection**, not launch longitude. The campaign has a QBO-dependent window, not a guaranteed season, and finding the right layer *is* the operation — a real decision a future strategic layer could surface, not solved-for-you weather.

**Glider, in detail.**
Sized for **mission-kill, not destruction** — a handful of 12.7 mm rounds through a rotodome, a pressure hull, or an engine is enough to force an enabler to abort or descend; you are not trying to blow it out of the sky. That collapses the mass budget from an armed-strike airframe down to ~140 kg, thrust = 0. Recoil on a thrustless glider is a real mechanic worth stating plainly: 12.7 mm fire costs roughly 7 kt/s of deceleration per burst; a 20 mm rotary cannon on the same mass would cost ~33 kt/s. Recoil is energy you cannot get back with a throttle you don't have.

**Balloon-lofted gliders and powered jet-drones are different lineages, not variants of one family.** Early drafts of this spec (and an early build) treated the glider as a thrust=0 configuration of the M1 fighter pair; it isn't. One-way, unpowered, soft-target strike gliders never dogfight and never re-attack; powered jet-drones are the BFM combatants. Keep them conceptually and mechanically separate — §4's derived family is the jet-drone lineage only.

## 4. Airframes — a derived family, two built first

**The generating principle:** cheap attritables *invert the multirole logic*. A manned 5th-gen must be multirole because it costs $100M and has to amortise across every mission; a $50k drone has no such pressure, so you build **narrow specialists**. The ecosystem is therefore a family, and every future airframe is justified by a mission-economics argument rather than invented. The design axes that actually trade against each other: **thrust vs wing** (energy vs angles), **high vs low altitude** (wing area for thin air vs strong small wing for gust loads), **speed**, and **range/endurance**. These map onto BFM archetypes *and* onto the threat ladder (§3a), because junk drones are low, gun-drones are mid, and manned fighters cruise high — so *where you can fight is what you built*, and choosing the fight's altitude becomes strategy one level above BFM.

**The M1 pair** (the archetypal BFM split — energy vs angles — built first because it's the fight's core dichotomy):

- **Type 1 — energy/vertical.** Cheapest possible kill: climb, convert height, snapshot, leave. T/W ~0.95, small high-loaded wing (~350 kg/m²), light structure. **Owns:** climb, acceleration, zoom, ceiling, the vertical. **Suffers:** sustained turn (bleeds viciously), sloppy slow, unforgiving. **Gun:** single ~30 mm cannon, ~600 rpm, ~90 rounds — one hit kills; a snapshot weapon. **Sensor:** cheap — narrow gimbal (~±90°), manual/stadiametric ranging (the player works the range). **Silhouette:** stubby, short-span, big intake, blunt — a flying engine with a cannon. Ugly and cheap.
- **Type 2 — angles/turn.** Air-superiority attritable: win the turning fight. T/W ~0.75, big wing (~220 kg/m²). **Owns:** sustained rate and radius, low-speed handling, transonic manners. **Suffers:** climb, acceleration, ceiling. **Gun:** ~20 mm rotary, ~3000 rpm, ~400 rounds — forgiving, but needs tracking *time*. **Sensor:** better — wide gimbal (~±150°), active ranging, computed gun solution. **Silhouette:** cleaner, longer swept wing, slimmer, prominent sensor ball where a canopy would be.

**Whose drones are these?** Type 1 is the PLA-side convergent shape (heavy snapshot cannon, crude sight, energy/vertical) and Type 2 is the Taiwan-side convergent shape (fast light gun, better sight, angles/turn) — both arrived at independently from the *same* cheap-attritable-mass economics, applied on opposite sides of the strait. That's the kind of convergent-evolution result real procurement produces when two forces face the same cost pressure from different starting points, not a coincidence written in for flavor. The setup screen lets you fly either (§3) — a 1v1 trainer/exhibition framing, not a claim that every real engagement is symmetric; Tier 2 (§3a) is explicitly asymmetric on purpose.

*Note where that lands:* vertical + heavy cannon + crude sight vs turn + fast light gun + good sight is **the same matchup shape as MiG-15 vs F-86, arrived at from cost and aerodynamics alone, with zero historical input.** The design space really is that constrained — worth recording as a finding, not worth naming the aircraft after it. Historical Sabre/MiG-15 performance data remains useful precisely as a **sanity anchor** (§5): if a derived airframe's numbers drift far from any jet that ever flew this envelope, that's a smell worth investigating. Type 1's manual-ranging sight keeps the mastery-track role the old MiG framing had (§9); nothing about that required calling it a MiG.

**Ammo is a live trade, and near-symmetric by accident:** 90 rds @ 600 rpm ≈ 9 s of trigger; 400 @ 3000 rpm ≈ 8 s. Both sides get ~8 seconds of gun per sortie. Burst discipline matters; more ammo costs mass costs performance.

**Shared envelope** (strawman, tunable): 5–7 m, 1500–3000 kg, single small turbojet, structural 12G (§5) — sustained G is thrust-limited *and* situational (§5); don't read a single number off this line as a spec, it depends on speed/altitude/T-W and is the M3 doctrine engine's call, not a constant. M0.85–0.92, 20–40 min endurance (fuel = the bingo round-timer, §3). No afterburner; energy is earned.

**Expansion axes (roadmap, not M1):** high-altitude specialist (big wing, altitude-lapsed engine — nearly free, the FDM already models density → q → G-available), low-altitude point-defence (small strong wing, gust loads), long-range hunter (fuel fraction vs knife-fight weight), short-range sprinter. **Delivery is a design axis too:** a balloon-lofted drone needs *no climb performance at all* (it's dropped at altitude — §3a), so it can spend that mass on wing or gun. The balloon-lofted glider (§3a) is a separate lineage, not an expansion axis of this family (see §3a).

## 5. Flight Dynamics Model (FDM)

**Custom 6DOF rigid body, table-driven aerodynamics.** Force/moment coefficients vs alpha, beta, Mach, and control deflections; mass/inertia/fuel-burn; engine thrust vs Mach/altitude with spool dynamics.

- **Data provenance — DERIVED, not archival, and it is a simplification.** The airframes are fictional, so there are no NACA/Soviet documents to identify parameters from and no rivet-counters to satisfy. Instead we **author each airframe to its derived envelope** (§4): pick wing/thrust/mass/structure from the mission-economics argument, then generate coefficient tables that hit the stated envelope. This *removes* the archival burden (chart digitisation, axis/CG-convention reconciliation, source-to-parameter coverage audit) — all of which existed only to serve historical fidelity we no longer claim.
  - **What replaces "is it historically right?" is "is it physically honest?":** every table must be internally consistent aerodynamics (sane polars, real induced-drag scaling, honest transonic rise), not curves reverse-fitted to make a fight fun. The flight-test cards below remain the gate — they just assert the *derived* envelope instead of a published one.
  - **The integrator/machinery validation stays exactly as specified:** validate the table machinery against the public **NASA TP-1538 F-16 model** (full tables, published check cases, JSBSim as cross-oracle) *before* authoring any airframe. That step was never about a real fighter's identity; it settles "is my sim right" independent of "is my data right," and it matters more now that the data has no external oracle.
  - Real-aircraft data (F-86/MiG-15 published performance) remains useful as a **sanity anchor** only — if a derived airframe's numbers drift far from any jet that ever flew this envelope, that's a smell worth investigating.
- **Compressibility is gameplay:** staged transonic degradation (snaking → buffet → pitch-up → control loss) must emerge from the tables, and is a per-airframe character trait — a cheap airframe with crude aerodynamics degrades earlier and nastier than a refined one. This is a *design lever*, not a historical fact to reproduce.
- **Departures and spins:** post-stall table extensions and departure tendencies modeled. Per the control grammar (§7), **bare arrows can never depart** — the drone's flight AI holds the envelope. Departure is reachable *only* by holding the override key, and then recovery is **uncertain**: correct rudder-led inputs give a real chance, some entry modes are unrecoverable. Departures are a weapon you bait the other guy into precisely *because* the outcome is not guaranteed. This model does not exist yet (M1 — see below, and the note on override "teeth").
- **G-limit: structural, not physiological — with the numbers now measured, not asserted.** No pilot aboard, so the ~9G human cap is gone; the limiter is the airframe. Measured on the placeholder reference airframe (the same methodology `docs/research/drone-derivation-brief.md` specifies for every future derived airframe): the **wing binds before the structure at every fighting speed** — aero-max G is 7.9G @ 389 kt, 11.4G @ 467 kt, 15.5G @ 544 kt; **20G would need 618 kt (M~0.97)**, well past what a cheap transonic airframe reaches. Structural limit is set at 12G — the honest build number; more than that is spar mass you can never use, because the wing gives out first. **Sustained G is separately thrust-limited** (only ~3.6G at 467 kt for the reference airframe's T/W of 0.39): removing the pilot buys *duration* at the aero limit, not a higher peak, and this is exactly what keeps the energy game alive (§1's "1950s-shaped" convergence — kill the tension between sustained and aero-max and the energy game dies with it).
  - **Consequence worth flagging, not hiding:** with the physiological cap gone, bare-arrow protection (`MaxPerformG`, 0.92× the aero limit) and the override's ceiling (`HardMaxG`, the aero/structural limit itself) sit only about 8% apart at a given speed. Riding bare arrows already gets you 92% of the way to the wing's honest limit — so the override's real teeth cannot come from "a bit more G." They have to come from the **M1 post-stall/departure model** above: riding past CLmax into buffet and possibly departure is genuinely different from riding at CLmax even though the G number barely moves. If the override still feels toothless once post-stall behavior lands, that's the actual bug to chase — not the size of the G gap.
  - **Tactical G is not a constant, and this spec should stop trying to make it one.** What G is *appropriate* right now — vs the aero max, vs sustained — is situational: it depends on his state, the energy account, rate-vs-radius, guns defense. That judgment is exactly what the M3 doctrine engine's two layers exist to make (§6). A "cap the pursuit laws at sustained G" attempt was tried during the M0 build and reverted for precisely this reason — flattening tactical G into a formula threw away the decision the whole engine exists to make. The sustained-G computation itself stays a physics quantity (a real energy-neutral turn rate, useful for scoring and possibly a HUD cue) and must never become a cap.
  - **Consequence for the AI:** its constraint is airframe and sensor (§6 belief state), never pilot tolerance — so difficulty remains behavior-only (§10), and "how hard does it hold the override" becomes a personality axis in its own right (§10), replacing the physiology dial that no longer exists.
- **Two models, one truth:** the full 6DOF drives the live fight. A **reduced-order companion model** (point-mass energy/geometry, the same approach the real-time NASA AML used) drives doctrine rollouts (§6); an M1 test asserts the reduced model's predictions track the 6DOF within stated bounds on the maneuver library.
- **Integration:** own fixed-timestep RK4 (120 Hz baseline), sim state **float64 end-to-end** with hand-rolled vector math inside the pure module (Godot's float32 Vector3 never touches sim state); Godot appears only in the render/collision bridge, which also owns world-frame rebasing. Jolt handles collision queries only — and anything sim-authoritative (ground, projectile hits) uses deterministic data queries, never camera-dependent physics.
- **Atmosphere:** the isothermal stratosphere (11–20 km, 216.65 K) is modeled explicitly, not just the troposphere — a troposphere-only extrapolation claimed 169 K instead of 216.65 K at 60,000 ft, a 13% Mach error exactly where balloon drops (§3a) live. Balloon missions forced this correctness fix; it now applies to every airframe.

### FDM validation — the flight test cards

Two stages, so a red card is debuggable:

1. **Integrator/machinery validation (before any airframe-specific data exists):** run the table machinery against a *complete public dataset with published check cases* — the NASA TP-1538 F-16 model — with JSBSim as a cross-oracle. Settles "is my sim right" independent of "is my data right."
2. **Aircraft cards:** level accel, max speed vs altitude, sustained turn rate/radius, climb schedules, transonic staging (per airframe), stall speeds — split into declared fit-targets and held-out validation. Spin cards are **qualitative** (recovers with correct inputs at plausible rates; sane altitude loss; entry modes match accounts).

Cards run headless in CI on every FDM/data change. Feel work (buffet, audio, shake) is authored on top of the validated model, never by bending it.

## 6. Doctrine Engine — one brain, two layers, three consumers

The component that knows *the textbook move*. Review exposed two missing pieces — the perception boundary and the selector/execution split — both now load-bearing:

**Layer 1 — the selector (slow, ~1–2 Hz):** NASA-AML-lineage trial-maneuver selection: generate candidates from a Shaw/P-825-derived maneuver library, forward-simulate a few seconds on the **reduced-order model** (never the full 6DOF — that's how the real AML was real-time), score on relative geometry + energy, output the best candidate *and* the live forks. Pinned budget (tuned at M3, enforced in CI): ~8–12 candidates, 3–5 s horizon, 10–20 Hz rollout step, opponent modeled as extrapolate-current-maneuver (never nested best-response — that squares the cost).

**Layer 2 — the execution laws (fast, every physics tick):** each maneuver in the library carries a cheap closed-form control law (G-command / pursuit law / lift-vector schedule) that turns the selected maneuver into per-tick recommended inputs. This is what the detents settle into, what the reflex baseline flies, what rollout candidates use internally, and what the AI actuates. Without this layer a 2 Hz selector would stair-step the detent valley; with it, the valley is smooth and the rollouts are cheap.

**The perception boundary (pillar 3 made architecture):** the engine consumes a **belief state, not ground truth** — own-ship truth + a bandit estimate gated by the same visual rules the player lives under (tally/no-tally via the sensor's gimbal-limited line of sight — §8's ±150° yaw / ±90° pitch — masking by own airframe, sun, haze, range/contrast; memory decay and growing position uncertainty while no-tally; high-G/buffet degrades sensor stabilization and acquisition — an airframe-vibration effect, not a physiological one, §5). **All three consumers eat the belief state.** The AI can genuinely lose sight of you; sun-blind reversals work because the architecture makes them work.

**Outputs:** (1) recommended per-tick control input (the valley), (2) named tactical context ("lag for spacing," "solution developing"), (3) **the forks, surfaced not chosen** — where doctrine genuinely branches (one/two-circle, rate vs radius), the engine returns *multiple selectable valleys*.

**The player decision taxonomy (pillar 2 made explicit):**
- **Player-owned, always:** fork commits, pursuit-geometry selection (lead/pure/lag), shot decisions, disengage/KIO, over-limit demands.
- **Reflex-owned:** AoA/G protection, coordination, roll quantization, terminal pipper damping (§9), holding the *player's chosen* geometry between decisions.
- **Fork commitment mechanism:** at a surfaced fork the current valley holds a neutral "maintain" solution; the player commits by *flying* — a committing input toward one option (roll direction at the merge, pull-vs-unload at the rate/radius branch) selects it, and the suggestion UI confirms which branch is now live. The inference rule is: sustained input toward an option = selection; ambiguous input keeps "maintain."
- Consumer scoping: the *AI* consumer picks its own forks (that's its personality, §10); "never auto-picks" governs the two player-facing consumers.

**Open design question — valley depth (the review's deepest challenge, resolved by test at M0):** do the valleys encode *doctrine* (hold = the recommended maneuver's G — the original "reflexes toward the optimal move" concept) or *physics only* (valleys = limiter/coordination/airmanship; doctrine lives in the suggestion UI plus weak magnetism)? Two reviewers argue doctrine-deep valleys risk War-Thunder-Instructor syndrome — riding the baseline unpunished below the top AI tier, with the fight's feel collapsing to ~10 decisions and spectating between them. The counter-position: shallow valleys may discard exactly the "modulate off the reflex" feel the game is founded on. **Both variants ship in the M0 grammar slice behind a debug toggle (`F1`); the feel gate decides.** Either way, the spec adds the requirement the challenge exposed: **deviation from doctrine must pay legibly at every AI tier** (the sortie grade rewards deviation quality; mid-tiers punish repeated patterns within a fight, not just Honcho across fights).

**Determinism:** doctrine evaluation is deterministic given belief state + seeded personality RNG; the selector runs on a worker thread with results applied at a **fixed tick offset** — and since AI control streams are recorded like the player's (§11), replay never re-runs the doctrine engine at all.

## 7. Control system — the local-minima grammar

**Mental model:** the virtual stick lives in a potential landscape whose valleys are the recommended inputs (§6 execution laws). Keys express *intent*; the stick settles into the nearest valley; perturb off it and it relaxes back on release. Assistance as detents in control space — never autopilot. **You must actually press; the game never plays itself.** A small always-on **stick-position indicator** shows the valley and your perturbation (non-optional — the valley must always be legible, or settling reads as "the game fighting my inputs").

**The G vocabulary, defined (review: three quantities were conflated):**
1. **Valley G** — what the selected maneuver's execution law recommends (≤ 2 below).
2. **Max-perform G** — best honest available performance right now (the reflex layer's AoA/G protection boundary — "the limiter" is this assist boundary; this airframe has no pilot to protect, so the boundary exists purely to keep bare arrows from ever departing, §5).
3. **The hard limits** — aero/structural truth, with no physiology in it (§5). Beyond 2 lies buffet, and — once the M1 post-stall model lands — departure.

### Key grammar (v0.2 — locked at the M0 feel gate; controls as actually flown, §5/§7 code)

| Input | Function | Grammar |
|---|---|---|
| **↓ (DOWN arrow)** | **Pull** | hold = valley G (stick-back convention: on a real stick, pulling back is positive G, and back on the keyboard is DOWN) · while holding, ↑-taps ease in −0.5G sticky steps (release-and-recommit to undo) · release = settle back to baseline |
| **↑ (UP arrow)** | **Unload / push** | hold = ~0G |
| **SPACE** | **Override** | held = raises the ceiling from bare-arrow max-perform to the aero/structural hard max — pull into the buffet, and (once the M1 post-stall model lands) risk departure, at your own insistence. Bare arrows alone can never depart; this key is the only way past protection. |
| **← / →** | Roll | tap = intent-quantized roll (lift vector on / deliberately past the bandit; at a surfaced fork, roll direction = fork commitment) · hold = continuous |
| **A / D** | Rudder | coordination reflex untouched; manual for guns defense, scissors, spin recovery |
| **W / S** | Throttle | detented: idle ↔ cruise ↔ mil |
| **F** | Guns | fire (currently dry — §9) |
| **X** | Cage/uncage sight | cages the gyro to the fixed cross during hard maneuvering — real LCOS-style discipline, affects the sight model (§9) |
| **C / B** | Flaps / speed brakes | discrete, honest speed limits |
| **V** | Padlock toggle / reacquire | sensor-honesty rules (§8) |
| **K** | Knock it off | ends the fight, straight to debrief |
| **R** | Restart setup | instant "again" |
| **Trackpad drag** | Freelook / gimbal slew | scanning when not padlocked, within the sensor's gimbal limits (§8) |
| **Two-finger scroll** | View zoom | never modal, never contextual |
| **Ranging (Type 1 only)** | Dedicated detented input | discrete span brackets with snap + audio tick (see §9) — deliberately *not* hover-contextual scroll (a modal-input error factory at 5G); exact binding chosen at M0 with hands on hardware |

No gear key in v1 (fights start and end airborne; pillar 5 cuts it — key reserved). Arrows are **pull/unload, not nose-up/down**, and the mapping is DOWN=pull, UP=push — the M0 build's *original* placeholder mapping had this backwards, and a former fighter pilot flying it caught the error instantly by feel; it was inverted to match real stick convention. This isn't the invert-Y "look axis" debate (there's no such debate to have here) — it's a force axis with one correct real-world convention, and the game now uses it. In no-tally flight the modifiers keep their meaning against pure airmanship valleys (max-perform, past-protection, 0G); only the doctrine content drops out.

*Positive off-valley modulation is deliberately absent: sticky ease-steps only go down, and the ceiling is reached through the separate Override key, not through a gesture on the pull key itself. Variant B (physics-only valleys) covers the upward range structurally, since its valley already sits at max-perform; variant A (doctrine-deep valleys) has no equivalent "ease up toward max-perform" gesture yet — whether it needs one is itself a live part of the A/B feel-gate question.*

### Hardware honesty (new, from review)

- **Chord budget: no mandatory simultaneous combination exceeds 3 keys.** Apple internal keyboards can electrically ghost on 3–4 key chords with no software fix; the actual grammar table gets a **day-one chord test on the target MacBook**, and the first-run calibration screen includes a rollover self-test.
- All tap/hold semantics run off the game's own timers on raw key events (OS key-repeat echoes filtered); trackpad gestures get a momentum deadband (macOS inertial scrolling keeps emitting deltas after finger-lift).

### Design principles

- **Pull is the default state of a dogfight;** the skill is modulation — when not to pull, when to demand more than the protection wants to give.
- **The interaction budget exists because the baseline flies.** With keys released the reflex holds competent, doctrine-shaped, *predictable* flight (it never shoots, never picks forks). Mid-fight trackpad work is legitimate, like trimming then reaching for the map.
- **The suggestion UI is a coach, not a pilot:** ghost key-prompts and fork options, fading with a player-set assist level down to off. The assist level touches the UI only — never the detents, never the indicator.
- **No tally, no doctrine:** without a padlocked bandit the detents relax to plain airmanship and the arrows become straightforward pitch/roll verbs.

## 8. Sight system — the flat-screen camera answer

- **Tally is computed at the drone's EO sensor aperture, not the render camera** — one rule for both aircraft (§6 belief state). The sensor is a gimbaled electro-optical head, clamped to **±150° yaw / ±90° pitch** (the M0-implemented limit); past that, the bandit is genuinely masked by your own airframe structure, not hidden by a UI rule. Within the gimbal: line of sight, airframe masking, sun, haze, range/contrast. Padlock (`V`) engages only with plausible tally; **breaks after a few seconds masked, gimbal-limited, or lost to high-G sensor-stabilization degradation** (buffet shaking the gimbal — an airframe effect, not a pilot blacking out, §5); reacquisition needs the bandit back inside the gimbal's field. Losing sight is real and loses fights.
  - **Per-airframe gimbal is a roadmap differentiation, not yet built.** §4 derives Type 1 as cheap/narrow (~±90°) and Type 2 as better/wide (~±150°); M0 ships one shared gimbal limit for both sides as a placeholder. Splitting it per airframe is straightforward but undone.
- **The presentation sells the fiction:** in the maneuver view, a bandit your *sensor* can't see visually degrades — drops smart-scaling, fades into haze — even if the raw geometry would put him on screen. What you see always agrees with what the sensor knows. This is an explicit M2 gate criterion.
- **FPV always — this is the whole view model now, not a mode.** The external orbit camera described in earlier drafts of this spec was deleted from the code outright (an explicit author decision), not merely left unused: there is no camera state that shows either drone from outside itself. What remains, and it's still two states meeting at one seam: the **maneuver view** — attitude-rigid first-person nose-cam anchored to own airframe as the attitude reference, the sensor head slewing (trackpad drag / padlock) independently of the airframe's own attitude — and the **gun view** — as a firing solution develops (range + angular-error thresholds), the view auto-blends into an honest collimated-sight frame for terminal tracking and the shot, blending back out as the solution collapses. Firing is allowed from any view; *aimed* fire happens through the sight.
- **SA strip** (Falcon 4 descendant): nose vs sensor-boresight vs bandit — and it **ages and suppresses its bandit cue when tally is lost** (a stale, widening "last seen" arc, not a live position — otherwise the strip is the magic sensor pillar 3 bans).
- **Smart scaling** so detection/tally distances match plausible sensor resolution rather than pixels. Haze, sun and the one optional cloud layer are fight variables, not cosmetics; sun-blind reversals work (§6 makes them real) — an EO sensor is dazzled by the same sun a human eye would be.

There is no G-physiology on the view — an EO sensor doesn't grey out or black out; that model was deleted along with the rest of pilot physiology (§5). Whether sustained high G should degrade sensor tracking quality beyond the padlock-break rule above is an open question, not a committed system — don't build it until it's actually designed.

## 9. Gunnery & damage — earned kills

- **Type 2 (auto-ranging sight):** the sensor supplies range automatically once locked; wingspan is auto-set in a known 1v1 (a ritual with no decision is not a control — pillar 5). The pipper **lags and settles like a real lead-computing sight**; caged/uncaged (`X`) is a real skill with real sight behavior.
- **Type 1 (manual bracket-ranging sight):** manual stadiametric ranging as a **chunky, detented bracket mechanic** — discrete span brackets, snap + audio tick, readable at high G — worked through the fight. **The intended rhythm is written down: range in lag, refine approaching the saddle, shoot.** Ranging quality is scored in the debrief; a well-ranged 30 mm hit is the game's single most spectacular event, with its own gun-camera treatment. Type 1 is positioned in the setup screen as the mastery track. Hand-feasibility of the full Type-1 ranging loop is an M0 gate item.
- **No always-correct lead line, ever** (the War Thunder de-skiller is the named anti-pattern).
- **Terminal damping, with a written authority envelope (review: "steady hand, not aimbot" must be defined, not asserted):** inside a small angular error with a developing solution, the reflex may **damp player-commanded tracking oscillation only** — it never adds lead, never acquires, corrects at a bounded rate, and only acts while the player is actively tracking. The player's contribution to a kill is the geometry *and* the tracking; the assist removes hand-tremor a human arm would have had and this airframe's actuators don't, nothing else.
- **Ballistics:** integrated projectiles (gravity drop, velocity inheritance, per-gun dispersion), pooled and substepped **in the native kernel** (a burst is ~150 live projectiles — a second hot path, budgeted, not discovered). Tracers render honestly and tip off the defender — burst visibility vs. lethality is a real tradeoff both the player and the AI live under.
- **Damage:** per-part — engine, controls (per-surface), structure, fuel (leak/fire). **No pilot channel:** there's no one aboard, so structural/control/engine/fuel loss is the entire kill model, and there is no ejection ending — §3's endings list reflects this: a departed, unrecoverable airframe is a loss, not an ejection.
- **The glider is a different weapon on a different airframe, not a variant of the gun above (§3a).** 140 kg, thrust = 0, 12.7 mm sized for mission-kill (a rotodome, a pressure hull, an engine) rather than destruction. Recoil is a real mechanic on a thrustless airframe: 12.7 mm costs ~7 kt/s of deceleration per burst; a 20 mm rotary on the same mass would cost ~33 kt/s — recoil is energy you cannot get back with a throttle you don't have.
- **Guns defense is a real skill:** out-of-plane jinks (unload, rudder) against a tracking solution, physics deciding what saves you.
- **Status, honestly: the gun is dry.** M0 wires the trigger, the tracking geometry, and "shot taken" telemetry (`bin/mission` reports gun-window opens and pulls-inside-window); it does not yet fire a projectile, apply damage, or resolve a kill. That's M4 (§14).

## 10. Adversary AI — the actual product

- **Doctrine engine + personality layer**, consuming the **belief state** (§6) — the AI maintains tally like you do, loses you behind its own tail, gets sun-blinded, and its sensor degrades under buffet before its controls do.
- Personality: **decision quality** (how often it picks the best candidate vs plausible-inferior), **fork character** (aggressive/conservative at the branches), **timing error** (reaction latency), **gunnery error** (aim noise, burst discipline, range judgment), **override willingness** (how readily it holds the override past protection, §5/§7 — the unmanned-airframe replacement for a physiology dial: risk tolerance for buffet and possible departure, not G-tolerance of a body that isn't there), **KIO/disengage policy** (per tier, plus the fuel-driven risk-acceptance term from §3).
- **Difficulty tiers are behavior, never *airframe* performance:** Rookie → Veteran → Ace → Honcho. All tiers bleed energy identically because they fly the same FDM through the same control layer. Mid-tiers punish *repeated patterns within a fight*; Honcho reads your habits — persistently across fights as a stretch goal, making the top tier a rival, not a setting.
- **M3 acceptance gates, telemetry-verified: the AI never energy-cheats *and never information-cheats*** (its shot/maneuver decisions are reproducible from its logged belief state, never from ground truth it shouldn't have).
- **No RL.** AlphaDogfight behaviors (frame-perfect forward-quarter snapshots, zero self-preservation) are what makes an opponent feel cheap. Scripted doctrine + personality noise is the design.

## 11. Debrief — the other half of the sortie

- **Replay = recorded control streams for both aircraft + seed** (the AI's stream recorded exactly like the player's — replay never re-runs the doctrine engine), with periodic snapshots **for scrubbing/seek, not correction**. Replays are stamped with sim-build + data-table hashes; the determinism guarantee is scoped (same build, same machine — CI runs replay-hash tests; cross-version playback resyncs from snapshots).
- **Debrief view:** 3D flight-path ribbons colored by specific energy, timeline scrubber, gun events with hit maps, G/AoA/airspeed/altitude traces, **input overlay on by default** (deviation moments must read — also the marketing answer to "it looks like it plays itself": the trailer is the debrief and the gun camera, not live externals — and there *are* no live externals anymore, §8).
- **Doctrine annotations:** advantage-flip markers, named maneuvers, the fork where you chose wrong — the anti-"died without knowing why" system. First appearance of each term links its concept card (§3). Non-kill endings are scored from the continuous advantage metric.
- **Gun camera film** for kills — a well-ranged shot deserves to be watched back.

## 12. World, shell, and presentation

- **Arena: bounded, fixed-origin** (review: Terrain3D cannot be origin-shifted — feature declined by its maintainers; the original origin-shifting plan was internally contradictory). Fights start near map center; the fight bubble (±~20 km, enforced by the §3 separation rules) keeps float32 render error sub-visible — verified by a ULP check in the M0 slice. Sim state is float64 world-frame regardless (§5); the bridge owns any future rebasing.
- **Taiwan Strait-flavored coastal terrain** (mountains, a strait crossing, coast), one optional cloud layer, time-of-day presets. Terrain look-from-altitude prototyped in M0. Altitude changes the sky, not just the terrain: above ~96% of the atmosphere the sky must actually read as near-space rather than a hazy afternoon, and getting that right (horizon falloff sharpening with altitude, sea/far-plane distance matching the true geometric horizon at ~70,000 ft, sun-tonemap tuning) took real rendering fixes found by looking at frames, not by trusting the shader on paper — see §13's testing note.
- **Look:** clean and legible over photoreal — "gun camera meets modern indie clarity." Bandit readability beats scenery, always.
- **Sound is half the physics feel:** spool, airflow vs IAS, buffet onset, gunfire with distance-appropriate report. Stylize where real data reads flat (the AC7 lesson).
- **Shell:** setup → fight → debrief, flight school drills, daily seeded fight, options (assist level, remap, first-run calibration incl. rollover self-test). Nothing else in v1.

## 13. Technical architecture

- **Engine:** Godot 4.7.x, macOS-native (Apple Silicon primary), open-source stack.
- **Languages (review: the hot-path arithmetic condemns GDScript-only *now*, not after profiling):** the **sim kernel — FDM, reduced-order model, rollouts, execution laws, ballistics — ships in C# from day one** (Godot .NET runs fine on Apple Silicon; no scons/godot-cpp toolchain pain), with float64 state and hand-rolled vector math. GDScript orchestrates: game shell, UI, camera, input grammar, doctrine *personality* glue. The kernel is a pure module (plain data in/out, headless-runnable); the whole rollout loop lives on one side of the language boundary (per-call marshalling 1,600× per decision is the trap).
- **Sim loop:** fixed-timestep kernel decoupled from render with interpolation; selector on a worker thread, decisions applied at fixed tick offsets (§6).
- **Headless-first testing, three tools answering three different questions:**
  - `bin/mission <scenario>` runs a scenario headless, no rendering, and reports a **mission-level verdict** — min range, whether a gun window ever opened, trigger pulls vs. pulls-inside-window. ~60× faster than rendering; for "did the intercept work" questions that are pure physics.
  - `bin/rig <scenario>` runs the same scenario through Godot movie-mode frame capture; for "does it *look* right" questions telemetry alone can't answer.
  - `bin/replay` re-renders a real recorded session (human or rig-driven) through `bin/rig` unchanged — the black-box recorder in `bridge/SimBridge.cs` is always-on, so any human flight becomes a re-renderable scenario for free.
  - Plus doctrine CI: invariants + statistical envelopes over many seeds (no energy/information cheats, limits respected, fights terminate, no NaNs — never single-fight outcome envelopes, which are flaky by construction in a chaotic system), with explicit re-baselining when tables intentionally change. Replay-hash tests.
- **Lesson worth stating plainly, because it cost real time to learn: code gates are not gameplay gates.** 90 unit tests and two-model adversarial review passed while the game was, for stretches of the build, an unflyable void — the pull was invisible, the pitch ladder rolled against the horizon, the edge of the world was visible at 70,000 ft, and the flagship Tier-0.5 mission was kinematically impossible (§3a). Every one of those was found by **rendering and looking, or by flying** — `bin/rig`, `bin/mission`, or a human at the keyboard — never by a passing test suite. Keep building headless tests; don't mistake a green suite for proof the game plays.
- **Determinism discipline:** seeded RNG everywhere, no wall clock in sim, fixed iteration order, recorded input streams as the replay format, scoped guarantee (§11).
- **Repo:** `~/Projects/guns-only`; this spec + the research survey + review findings committed.

## 14. Milestones — feel risk first, fidelity second (review: the original order gated the core bet behind three milestones that didn't test it)

**Status as of 2026-07-17:** M0 is built (90 tests green) but the feel gate itself has not been flown — `docs/m0-gate.md`'s checklist is unstarted. What headless testing has already found, honestly: **2 of the 3 core BFM beats never reach a firing solution today** — the hand-authored M0 execution laws fly toward the bandit but don't converge to a tracking window. That is not a bug; it is exactly the gap the M3 doctrine engine exists to close (§3a, §6). The gun itself is **dry** (§9) — no ballistics, no damage, no kill — until M4. Beat 4 (the glider strike) needs its geometry redesigned around a diving slashing pass, not the tail-chase it currently attempts (§3a).

- **M0 — the grammar slice (THE feel gate).** Placeholder reduced-order flight model (honest energy *shape*, no table fidelity), flat sea arena, scripted-rail bandit, **hand-authored doctrine stream** (canned execution laws for 3–4 scenario beats: perch attack, break defense, saddle + shot, balloon strike), full detent grammar, padlock + both view states, stick indicator, suggestion prompts. Plus the hardware spikes: **chord/ghosting test on the target MacBook, gesture momentum spike, terrain-from-altitude look, render ULP check.** **Gate: combat detents feel like flying BFM — tested in both valley-depth variants (§6) — and the hands physically work on the keyboard. Nothing else gets built until this gate passes; explicit willingness to redesign the grammar here.**
- **M1 — the honest airplane.** M1a: kernel + table machinery validated against the public NASA F-16 dataset with JSBSim cross-oracle. M1b: derive envelopes for both airframe types (§4, §5) → fit-target + held-out cards green. Reduced-order model fidelity test vs the 6DOF. **Post-stall/departure modeling lands here too** — it's what gives the override key (§7) real teeth beyond the ~8% G gap between protected and hard-max (§5). Swap into the slice; **re-gate feel** (the grammar must survive contact with the real airplane).
- **M2 — the sight fight.** Eyeball-honesty rules, belief-state tally (player side), presentation-sells-masking, SA strip with aging cue, smart scaling, canned-maneuver drone. Flight school drills ride this milestone (same machinery). Beat 4's real mission — a diving slashing pass, not a tail chase (§3a) — gets properly designed here, not patched in M0. *Gate: losing sight is scary; what you see always matches what the sensor knows.*
- **M3 — the brain.** Two-layer doctrine engine (selector + execution laws), belief state for the AI, personality tiers, fork surfacing/commitment. Closes the "canned laws don't converge to a shot" gap found in M0 (above). *Gates: an honest AI you beat by out-deciding; energy-cheat AND information-cheat telemetry clean; rollout budget holds real-time.*
- **M4 — the kill.** Both sights (Type 2 auto-range + caging; Type 1 bracket ranging), ballistics, damage, guns defense, debrief with doctrine annotations + input overlay + gun camera. This is where the gun stops being dry (§9).
- **M5 — the matchup.** Type 1 + Type 2 tables and cards finalized (§4, §5), setup screen, fight-ending rules tuned, daily seeded fight, sortie grading, sound/feel polish. The thin strategic layer (blockade posture shaping which tactical setups are on offer, §3a) is the retention-hole answer and lands here or later — undesigned.

## 15. Risks

1. **The detent grammar is unproven anywhere.** The gate for this risk is M0 **by design** — but as of 2026-07-17 it has not actually been flown (`docs/m0-gate.md` checklist unstarted), so this risk is still open in fact, not just in theory. Fallbacks if the gate fails: shallower valleys, continuous-hold analog emulation, coarser detents. The one-brain architecture survives any grammar outcome.
2. **Valley depth is an open design question** (doctrine-deep vs physics-only): resolved by A/B at the M0 gate; the requirement that deviation pays at every tier stands regardless.
3. **Derived envelopes have no external oracle.** Because the airframes are fictional (§5), there is no archival dataset to check a derived table against. "Is it physically honest" is self-graded against the flight-test cards and the TP-1538/JSBSim machinery validation, plus real F-86/MiG-15 performance data as a sanity anchor only. That's a real methodology, not a weaker one — but it means an internally-consistent, unconvincing table has no external check to catch it. (This replaces v3's claim that MiG data needed archival parameter identification — that plan no longer applies; there is no MiG.)
