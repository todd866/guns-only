# World research memo: Korea, 1950s / 2030s

Status: research and setting proposal, not canon
Research cutoff: 19 July 2026

## The useful version of the premise

`Guns Only` can use one peninsula to tell two different stories about the same problem: how a pilot makes a defensible decision with incomplete information, finite fuel, fallible machinery, political boundaries and weather that does not care about the plan.

- The 1950-1953 campaign should remain recognizably historical: a limited war under coalition command, an abrupt transition from fluid operations to stalemate, sanctuary and border restrictions around the Yalu, primitive jet endurance, straight-deck naval aviation, early helicopter evacuation, harsh terrain and weather.
- The 2030s campaign is alternate history. Its initiating event—a rapid PRC fait accompli over Taiwan without open US-PRC war—is a chosen dramatic premise, **not a forecast and not a claim about likelihood**.
- Korea is not merely the next square on a US-China board. The ROK and DPRK are principals with their own interests, red lines, institutions and capacity to surprise their sponsors. A convincing proxy war is one the supposed proxies can escalate, obstruct or redirect.
- The visual and physical setting should not be “the 1950 map with drones.” South Korea's denuded postwar hills were extensively reforested and its cities transformed. The same ridgelines can support cross-era echoes, but land cover, roads, skylines, light, electromagnetic clutter and civilian density must be era-specific.

The tone should be: **two eras, one terrain skeleton, different human worlds, the same burden of judgment**.

## Fact / inference / fiction boundary

### Established history or present-day policy

- North Korean forces crossed the 38th parallel on 25 June 1950. The UN Security Council called for their withdrawal in Resolution 82; later resolutions recommended assistance to the ROK and created a US-led unified command. The US Air Force's official history divides the war into the northern advance, the UN reversal after Inchon, Chinese intervention, and a long stalemate.
- The 27 July 1953 agreement is an armistice, not a peace treaty. It established the Military Demarcation Line, DMZ and Military Armistice Commission. It was signed by military commanders for the UN Command, Korean People's Army and Chinese People's Volunteers; the ROK was not a signatory. The armistice still structures access, investigation and communication around the DMZ.
- The US-ROK Mutual Defense Treaty treats an armed attack in the Pacific area on either party in territories under its recognized administrative control as a common danger, with each acting through its constitutional processes. It also provides for US land, air and sea forces in and about the ROK by mutual agreement.
- The Taiwan Relations Act is materially different. It makes coercion against Taiwan a grave concern, provides for defensive articles and directs the President and Congress to determine an appropriate US response through constitutional processes; it is not an automatic mutual-defense trigger.
- PRC law and policy state a preference for peaceful reunification while reserving “non-peaceful means” under specified conditions. Taiwan's own defense policy emphasizes asymmetric capability, decentralization, resilience and whole-of-society defense. Neither body of policy establishes how a future crisis must end.
- China and the DPRK signed a Treaty of Friendship, Cooperation and Mutual Assistance in 1961, which Beijing still describes as a political and legal foundation of the relationship. China's stated Korean Peninsula policy emphasizes peace, stability and a political settlement. Those facts do not make DPRK and PRC preferences identical.
- Current ROK and US policy already points toward AI-enabled crewed-uncrewed teaming, autonomy, electromagnetic-spectrum operations, resilient command networks and industrial-base cooperation. Current US programs likewise emphasize numerous attritable uncrewed systems. These are directional anchors, not reliable specifications for 2034 equipment.

### Reasonable setting inferences

- An explicit US-ROK treaty, forward-deployed forces, UNC mechanisms and rear-area access in Japan would make a Korean crisis politically and operationally different from a Taiwan crisis even in a world where Washington declined to fight over Taiwan.
- Beijing would strongly prefer not to see hostile forces approach the Yalu/Tumen border, loose nuclear material, uncontrolled refugees or an allied unified Korea. It would also have reasons to restrain a DPRK action that could pull China into direct war. These are plausible interests, not guaranteed decisions.
- Washington and Beijing could try to keep uniformed forces from directly engaging while supplying local forces with intelligence, software, matériel, training, sustainment and remotely supported systems. Such a proxy structure would be unstable, leaky and contested—not a perfect firewall.
- Dense autonomous forces would make bandwidth, electrical power, software configuration, batteries, spares, calibration, spectrum access and trusted identification as important as the airframe count.

### Pure fiction to be declared in the game

- The timing, mechanism and result of the Taiwan fait accompli.
- Any later political settlement or de facto acceptance of PRC control.
- The “West Sea Blackout,” the chain of incidents that restarts fighting in Korea, and every 2030s organization, operation and platform.
- Any claim that the ROK outproduces the United States in theater-useful drones; this is a strong
  setting choice to model, not a numerical forecast derived from present procurement totals.
- Any agreement by Washington and Beijing to limit direct combat, and every case in which that agreement holds or fails.

The setting bible should tag every 2030s claim as `historical anchor`, `author inference` or `fictional event`. That lets writers change the future without silently rewriting the real past.

## A concise divergence timeline

The dates are deliberately movable. The causal sequence matters more than a precise year.

### 2028 — The Nine-Day Strait Crisis (fiction)

A large “customs and safety” exercise around Taiwan becomes a coercive quarantine. Cable failures, cyber disruption, contradictory mobilization orders, market closure and selective physical seizures make it impossible for Taipei and outside governments to establish a common picture. An emergency political accord is accepted before a conventional invasion or direct US-PRC exchange begins. PRC forces enter designated ports, airfields and government facilities under that accord; resistance is localized rather than organized into a sustained campaign.

There is no declared US-PRC war. Whether the episode counts as “peaceful” is bitterly contested by people who experienced coercion and scattered violence. Beijing calls it national reunification; many Taiwanese call it capitulation under siege; foreign ministries use intentionally bloodless language.

This should **not** be written as “Taiwan was weak” or “China was destined to win.” In this branch, political synchronization, communications and decision thresholds fail faster than Taiwan's material defenses can become decisive.

### 2028-2030 — De facto acceptance, legal ambiguity (fiction built on real policy distinctions)

The United States, Japan and other governments accept the immediate reality rather than begin a great-power war. Acceptance of control is not universal legal recognition. Sanctions, export controls, refugee and citizenship disputes, commercial compromises and competing historical narratives persist.

The result produces opposite lessons:

- Beijing concludes that calibrated coercion, information isolation and a short decision window can change facts before a coalition forms.
- Washington's allies conclude that ambiguous commitments are vulnerable and demand visible, executable guarantees.
- Seoul stresses that the US-ROK treaty and forces already in Korea must remain distinct from the former Taiwan posture.
- Pyongyang concludes that confusion below the threshold of acknowledged war creates opportunity.

### 2030-2032 — The alliance audit (fiction)

The ROK disperses aviation, hardens civil-military networks and accelerates crewed-uncrewed teams. The United States moves more sustainment and command capacity into distributed regional locations. Japan expands rear-area support while retaining national political and legal caveats. China seeks to consolidate the post-Taiwan order without accepting an allied buildup on its Korean border. DPRK and Russia deepen selective technical and logistics ties; Beijing cannot assume it controls either partner.

### 2033 — The West Sea Blackout (fiction)

A long period of GNSS anomalies, jamming and communications outages around the Northwest Islands culminates in uncrewed surface and air contacts crossing disputed operating lines. A ROK sensor site and a DPRK coastal battery are both struck. Each side publishes convincing but incomplete telemetry implicating the other. The armistice hotline works intermittently, and authentic messages are mixed with fabricated ones.

No omniscient narrator reveals the first attacker. Later missions can accumulate evidence without resolving every ambiguity.

### 2034 — Korea, round two (fiction)

A limited DPRK ground action and ROK counteraction turn the armistice into active but geographically bounded combat. The United States responds under the alliance at Seoul's request. China supplies and shields the DPRK while publicly demanding a ceasefire and attempting to keep US and PRC uniformed units from direct contact. Both powers use advisers, national technical means, contractors, licensed production and machine-speed intelligence support; both deny that this makes them belligerents.

The campaign begins after deterrence has failed but before anyone believes escalation is uncontrollable. The player lives in that dangerous interval.

## Why Korea becomes the arena

### 1. An unfinished war already supplies institutions and tripwires

The armistice remains a living military agreement, with a demarcation line, controlled corridors, investigations, hotlines and multinational machinery. That creates unusually rich gameplay: a contact can be tactically obvious but legally ambiguous; recovering a downed drone can matter more than destroying it; an accurate shot can still be a strategic failure.

### 2. The two US relationships are not interchangeable

The setting does not need “Taiwan is far from CONUS” as its causal engine. Both theaters are trans-Pacific from the continental United States. Distance matters to logistics, but the more important contrast is the combination of an explicit US-ROK treaty, forces already present, combined planning and access to UNC rear support. The Taiwan Relations Act preserves deliberate political choice; the US-ROK treaty and posture create different expectations, tripwires and sunk commitments.

Cuba is therefore a mood analogy at most, not proof that geography determines intervention. Proximity, treaty language, deployed forces, nuclear risk, domestic legitimacy, coalition access and local agency all shape the decision.

### 3. Korea is immediately adjacent to China

The border makes instability, refugees, nuclear security and the disposition of forces direct Chinese security concerns. It also recreates the central dramatic geometry of the 1950s: action near the Yalu can be militarily tempting and politically forbidden.

### 4. Both Koreas are capable actors

The ROK brings a sophisticated military, dense civil infrastructure, a large technology and industrial base, and democratic political constraints. The DPRK brings hardened systems, nuclear escalation risk, internal opacity, massed conventional capability and a leadership whose objectives need not match Beijing's. Neither should be reduced to an American or Chinese avatar.

### 5. The terrain compresses modern systems into hard choices

Ridges create radar and radio shadows. Valleys channel movement and mask emitters. The western estuaries and islands mix military contacts with civilian fishing and shipping. Dense southern cities make collateral risk and infrastructure continuity unavoidable. Sea approaches allow carrier, escort, mine, drone-tender and logistics stories without making every mission a deep-ocean campaign.

## Coalitions: interests and constraints

### Republic of Korea

**Interests:** defend its population and territory; keep sovereign control of escalation; preserve ports, power, data and industry; prevent a nuclear exchange; maintain alliance credibility; decide the terms of any armistice or settlement.

**Constraints:** extraordinary civilian density; coalition intelligence and classification barriers; dependence on vulnerable networks and imported components; democratic scrutiny; divergent views about reunification, retaliation and acceptable risk.

**Writing rule:** Seoul requests, limits, refuses and bargains. It is never simply given a US mission list.

**2034 industrial premise (fiction):** the ROK has become the coalition's fastest mass producer of
theater-useful drones, batteries, actuators and replacement structures. Short supply lines and rapid
field iteration let Korean factories outproduce US programs in relevant airframes without implying
that the ROK exceeds total US industrial capacity. The US remains disproportionately important for
selected sensors, cryptography, relays, intelligence, strategic lift and alliance authority. This
imbalance creates gameplay: plentiful Korean hardware can still be grounded by a US crypto load,
classification boundary, software certification dispute or unavailable specialist component.

### United States and UNC contributors

**Interests:** meet treaty commitments; prevent allied defeat; preserve regional access and credibility; constrain DPRK nuclear use; prevent direct US-PRC war; keep a lawful multinational basis for operations.

**Constraints:** constitutional and coalition processes; finite theater stocks and maintenance capacity; long and attackable logistics; partner caveats; dependence on data-sharing permissions; the danger that a “defensive” action is interpreted as regime-change preparation.

**Writing rule:** US sensors do not confer universal truth. A track may be hidden from a partner, released too late, classified incorrectly or based on a source that cannot be exposed.

### Japan

**Interests:** prevent missile and maritime threats; keep sea lanes open; support US and ROK security; protect its own bases and population.

**Constraints:** domestic politics and law; base vulnerability; historic mistrust with Korea; mission-specific caveats. The 2023 trilateral commitment is to consult and coordinate, not a single automatic command structure.

### Democratic People's Republic of Korea

**Interests:** regime survival; coercive leverage; fracture the US-ROK alliance; obtain technology and resources; preserve freedom of action from Beijing and Moscow; control the story told to its population.

**Constraints:** sanctions and supply quality; fragile civil infrastructure; uncertain equipment readiness; dependence on foreign inputs; fear of decapitation or loss of nuclear control; sponsor pressure that may conflict with its own timetable.

**Writing rule:** DPRK commanders can conceal losses, disobey sponsor advice, exploit aid, or stop an operation that China expected them to continue.

### People's Republic of China

**Interests:** prevent allied forces moving toward its border; avoid DPRK collapse or loose nuclear materials; preserve influence over any settlement; demonstrate that the post-Taiwan regional order cannot be reversed; avoid a ruinous direct war with the United States.

**Constraints:** the DPRK is an ally, not a remote-controlled unit; military assistance risks attribution and escalation; northeast Chinese cities and infrastructure are exposed to spillover; economic and diplomatic costs grow with visible intervention; Russia can undercut Chinese restraint.

**Writing rule:** Beijing can simultaneously sustain the DPRK, threaten intervention and pressure Pyongyang to stop. Apparent contradiction is policy under competing risks, not necessarily bad writing.

### Russia and other spoilers

Current DPRK-Russia military cooperation and the 2024 partnership treaty make Russia a plausible supplier, intelligence actor and veto player. It need not become a third superpower campaign. Its best narrative role is to sell capability, distort attribution and make Beijing less able to control escalation.

## What drone warfare should actually feel like

The 2030s should not replace aerodynamics and uncertainty with a perfect tactical map. Official US and ROK programs point toward resilient networks, autonomy and crewed-uncrewed teaming precisely because jamming, scale and imperfect communications are hard problems.

### Tracks are claims, not objects

Every contact should carry:

- source or source class;
- observation time and track age;
- location and identity uncertainty;
- correlation history;
- confidence and possible deception flags;
- which coalition members can see it;
- whether it meets the current rule for engagement.

The player should be able to see why the system believes something, not merely whether a red diamond exists.

### A datalink is a consumable service

Model coverage, latency, congestion, authentication state, line of sight, relay health and emission risk. A disconnected drone should execute a bounded lost-link contract, not become either useless or magically omniscient. Reconnection can create duplicate tracks, conflicting tasking and stale weapons authority.

### Electronic warfare cuts both ways

Jamming can deny, degrade or deceive, but it consumes power, spectrum and attention and advertises an emitter. Friendly interference and poor spectrum management should be possible. Protection, frequency agility and passive sensing reduce risk; none should be absolute. GNSS denial should cause error growth and force inertial, terrain, celestial, visual or network-aided updates rather than switch navigation off.

### Autonomy is delegated authority

An autonomy package should be defined by geographic box, time limit, target class, confidence threshold, collateral constraints, weapons state and abort behavior. Human control means designing, authorizing, supervising and auditing those bounds; it need not mean manually steering every vehicle. Different governments and services should impose different bounds.

### Maintenance and configuration are combat power

Track battery cycles, motor or engine health, actuator margins, sensor calibration, contaminated optics, salt exposure, software versions, crypto loads, spare assemblies and cannibalization. A fleet of 200 nominal airframes may yield 83 launchable, 61 with current keys and 47 legal for the mission. That gap is where the maintenance-test-flight game lives.

### Logistics are targetable and visible

Dispersed operating locations need fuel or charging, munitions, data, tools, trained maintainers, weather protection, runway or pad repair and transport. Moving every night improves survivability but consumes readiness. A successful mission may be one that preserves a test set, recovers a damaged aircraft or gets the correct software baseline to a forward strip.

### ROE is part of the sensor model

Positive identification, protected sites, civilian movement, neutral shipping, national boundaries, the DMZ, the Yalu/Tumen border and nuclear facilities should alter what information is required before action. The most capable weapon may be unavailable because its provenance would expose a sponsor or because its target evidence cannot be shared.

## Terrain, cloud, wind and storm direction

Environment is not a graphics layer. It is an information and performance system shared by both eras.

### Build two land-cover worlds over one geographic skeleton

The US Army described the 1951 central peninsula as rugged, water-cut terrain with a primitive road network strongly affected by weather. The Korea Forest Service records that 1950s forests were severely devastated, followed by large-scale restoration; by 2020 average growing stock was 29 times the 1953 level. Official Army historical writing likewise notes that once-bare hills became dense forest and urban expansion made many old battle sites difficult to recognize.

Therefore:

- **1950s layer:** exposed or scrubby ridges, eroded slopes, rice paddies, dirt and narrow roads, rail/bridge chokepoints, small war-damaged settlements, sparse night lighting, low radio density.
- **2030s layer:** forested ridges, dense urban corridors, towers and tunnels, expressways and high-capacity rail, reservoirs and industrial areas, bright night clutter, civilian emitters everywhere.
- **Shared layer:** major relief, rivers, coastlines and recognizable passes. A player can recognize the same ridge while being surprised by everything humans built or regrew around it.

### Weather families grounded in Korea's climate

The Korea Meteorological Administration describes a strong seasonal contrast, the Changma rainy season beginning in late June and lasting about 30 days, frequent heavy rain and flash flooding, winter heavy snow, and typhoon influence from June to October. Its climate normals show prevailing northwesterly winter and southwesterly summer winds, with stronger coastal land-sea effects.

Use four authored weather families rather than random “bad weather”:

1. **Winter monsoon:** cold, dry continental flow; gusty northwesterlies; mountain wave and mechanical turbulence; snow showers and icing where moisture arrives; cold-soak starting and battery problems.
2. **Changma:** deep layered cloud, low ceilings, heavy rain, embedded convection, flooded valleys, poor optical/IR performance, runway contamination and large wind changes with height.
3. **Typhoon approach and recovery:** expanding wind field, squalls, pressure fall, sea state, runway closure, evacuation and post-storm reconstitution. The operational decision often occurs a day before the dramatic weather.
4. **Transition season:** clear dry air interrupted by fronts, strong visibility, sea-breeze reversals, fog/low cloud near coasts and large daytime heating differences over valleys and slopes.

### Minimum simulation contract

- 3D wind varying with position, altitude and time, including surface friction, terrain channeling, ridge lift/rotor, sea breeze, fronts, gusts and turbulence.
- Temperature, pressure and humidity profiles that drive density altitude, true/indicated airspeed relationship, engine or rotor performance, icing and cloud base.
- Cloud volumes with base, top, liquid/ice content, precipitation and convective strength—not a visibility toggle.
- Sensor-specific weather effects: optical contrast, infrared attenuation/background, radar clutter/attenuation where appropriate, laser range limits, radio propagation and lightning interference.
- Ground response: standing water, mud, snow, flood stage, sea state, runway braking and spray.
- Forecast uncertainty: the player receives a briefing and updates, not the simulation's hidden exact state.

## Cross-era thematic echoes

| 1950s problem | 2030s echo | Decision being tested |
| --- | --- | --- |
| F-86 endurance and short patrol time in MiG Alley | drone battery, relay endurance and tanker/charger availability | How long can I remain useful and still recover? |
| Yalu sanctuary and prohibition on crossing | geofenced weapons authority near the PRC border | Is a fleeting tactical gain worth strategic escalation? |
| Voice radio failure and visual formation discipline | jammed mesh, stale tracks and lost-link contracts | What can subordinates safely do without me? |
| Rail and bridge interdiction followed by rapid repair | decoys, robotic repair and distributed logistics | What effect did the attack actually buy? |
| Straight-deck recovery with damaged systems | recovery of a degraded autonomous aircraft on a drone tender or strip | Preserve the machine, the data, or the deck? |
| Early helicopter evacuation in terrain and weather | contested casualty evacuation and unmanned resupply | Who receives scarce lift, and when is risk justified? |
| Limited cockpit instrumentation and pilot inference | dense but contradictory machine-generated advice | More data does not remove the need for judgment. |
| Mechanical maintenance records | software, sensor and crypto configuration control | Is the aircraft genuinely mission capable? |
| Coalition language, training and doctrine differences | data sovereignty, classification and interoperability | Can allies form one usable picture? |
| Monsoon, snow and mountain obscuration | multi-sensor degradation and link shadow | Is weather cover, threat, or both? |

## Sortie and backstory hooks

1. **The Sanctuary Line (1951 / 2034).** A damaged opponent turns toward the Yalu. In 1951 the Sabre pilot must balance fuel, identification and the border restriction. In 2034 a correlated drone track approaches the same line while its identity confidence falls and its last weapons authorization expires. The debrief compares what each pilot knew at the decision point.

2. **Deck Five (1951).** A naval jet launches from a straight deck, develops a hydraulic or flap indication fault and must diagnose it without consuming the fuel needed for another recovery attempt. The sortie is a combat-capable maintenance test flight: observe, isolate, configure, communicate and recover.

3. **Angel in the Snow (1951).** A light helicopter crew must retrieve a critical casualty from a mountain clearing in marginal winter weather. Payload, density, wind, daylight, enemy observation and recurring maintenance faults compete. Historically, Korean helicopter evacuation grew through improvisation while suffering spare-parts, fuel and reliability problems.

4. **Monsoon Window (1952 / 2034).** A rail chokepoint appears through a short cloud break. The 1952 pilot has one pass before weather closes. The 2034 operator has several sensor feeds, but rain attenuation, decoys and an expiring civilian-traffic exclusion make the “better” picture less certain.

5. **West Sea Blackout (2033).** Uncrewed contacts converge on a ROK island radar site among neutral fishing traffic. GNSS is unreliable, the armistice hotline is intermittent and two coalition feeds disagree. The mission can end in interception, shadowing, recovery of wreckage, or an unjustified first shot.

6. **The Last Clean Track (2034).** A relay aircraft begins to fail. The player can keep it radiating to support a strike, send it home with irreplaceable mission data, or sacrifice it as a decoy. Every dependent track displays increasing age and uncertainty as the choice unfolds.

7. **Broken Compass (1950 / 2034).** A winter front closes the destination. One era uses dead reckoning, beacons and terrain; the other has a spoofed navigation solution and three disagreeing machine estimates. Both require the pilot to reject an apparently precise instrument.

8. **Cold-Soak Test Card (1951 / 2034).** The historical crew diagnoses slow engine acceleration, fuel-control behavior and hydraulic response after an overnight freeze. The modern team tests batteries, actuators, sensor boresight and software after a rushed field update. Passing the card releases several later sorties; signing off weak evidence plants a delayed failure.

9. **The Hotline (2034).** A surveillance drone crashes inside a controlled area of the DMZ. Recovering its crypto and data risks an armistice violation; leaving it risks exploitation; destroying it may be interpreted as an attack. The player prepares evidence and recommendations while political channels run in parallel.

10. **Typhoon Sortie (both eras).** The objective is not to fight the storm but to preserve combat power before it arrives: ferry aircraft, choose which damaged machines are flyable, distribute fuel and spares, protect records and decide where crews will reconstitute afterward.

11. **Escort Without a Flag (2034).** An unmarked autonomous transport approaches a declared humanitarian corridor with an escort whose emissions resemble a known PRC package. Provenance, intent and target status are different questions. Shooting may expose a sponsor; allowing passage may resupply the front.

12. **Two Instructors (campaign frame).** A 2030s test pilot studies the recorded decisions of a 1950s pilot who flew over the same terrain. The historical sortie never supplies a simple answer; it supplies evidence, consequences and a standard of reasoning. The campaign's grading system judges whether the player formed and revised a defensible mental model.

## Claims and shortcuts to avoid

- **Do not say a rapid PRC victory over Taiwan is highly likely, inevitable or easy.** It is the branch this story chooses. Current policy documents establish intentions and preparations, not outcomes.
- **Do not use “walkover” in neutral narration.** It can appear as in-world triumphalism or bitter self-criticism, but the setting should show coercion, institutional failure, local agency and contested memory.
- **Do not say “war never broke out” without qualification.** Say there was no declared interstate war or direct US-PRC combat. People subjected to blockade, seizure or violence may reasonably reject the cleaner description.
- **Do not equate de facto acceptance with legal recognition.** Governments can decline to reverse control by force while continuing sanctions, non-recognition or unofficial relationships.
- **Do not claim the Taiwan Relations Act either guarantees or rules out US intervention.** It deliberately leaves an appropriate response to constitutional decision-making.
- **Do not claim the US-ROK treaty is an automatic algorithm.** It commits the parties to act against common danger through constitutional processes and exists inside real politics and command arrangements.
- **Do not treat China-DPRK or DPRK-Russia treaties as remote-control links.** Trigger interpretation, attribution, prior aggression and political choice still matter.
- **Do not erase Korean agency by calling the conflict only “America versus China.”** That phrase can describe the strategic shadow, not the complete war.
- **Do not depict UNC as a standing UN army controlled day-to-day from New York.** Its historical authority and present multinational command arrangements are more specific.
- **Do not reuse 1950s land cover for the 2030s.** Reforestation, urban growth and infrastructure are among the most visible changes on the peninsula.
- **Do not make drones all-weather, maintenance-free or perfectly networked.** Mass creates more batteries, keys, motors, links, software and failure states to manage.
- **Do not make jamming a universal off switch.** It produces local, frequency- and geometry-dependent denial, deception, interference and counter-detection.
- **Do not grant either coalition an omniscient red/blue tactical picture.** Source, age, uncertainty, disclosure and rules for identification are the heart of the modern game.
- **Do not extrapolate 2030s named weapons from current brochure specifications.** Model durable functions and constraints so the fiction survives real procurement changes.
- **Do not present disputed historical victory ratios as settled world facts.** They are unnecessary to make MiG Alley compelling.
- **Do not use Cuba's political history as proof that distance from CONUS determines US behavior.** At most, use it to start a conversation about tolerance, escalation and spheres of influence; the legal, military and political cases differ.
